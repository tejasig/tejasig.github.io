Hi, I am Tejasi!

I am currently an undergraduate student pursuing a degree in Chemistry at St. Stephen’s College, University of Delhi, and I am drawn to questions about how biomolecules are organised and interact with one another. I am particularly interested in using computational tools to explore the structure hidden within complex systems, in a way that remains physically and chemically meaningful.

Working with biomolecular data has often involved translating physical systems into graph-based representations, where relationships between components can be studied systematically. This has led me to develop Python-based analysis pipelines to process biomolecular structural data, construct graph-based representations, and explore patterns that are not immediately visible from raw molecular coordinates. I have also been engaging with molecular dynamics simulations as a way to connect structural organisation with underlying physical motion and interactions at the atomic scale. Through this work, I have had to make explicit choices about connectivity, representation, and modelling assumptions, which has made questions of interpretability central to how I analyse results and draw conclusions.

Building these pipelines step by step has been an important part of how I learn, as it highlights both the strengths and limitations of different modelling choices. Alongside this, I have come to appreciate the role of mathematics and physics in shaping how problems in chemistry and biology are framed, and in clarifying the assumptions underlying computational approaches.

I am broadly interested in computational biophysics and molecular modelling, particularly in areas where ideas from physics, chemistry, and mathematics intersect. This page is a brief reflection of what I am currently exploring and the way I like to think about scientific work.
